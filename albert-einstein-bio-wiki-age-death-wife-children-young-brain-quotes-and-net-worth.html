    Albert Einstein Brain

    After Einstein’s death, his brain was removed by the pathologist who performed the autopsy, Thomas Harvey. Harvey kept the brain and took it with him when he moved to Kansas. He cut the brain into several pieces and gave them to other researchers to study.

    Some of the brain tissue was studied and found to be abnormal in some ways, including having unusually few glial cells, which support as well as protect neurons. However, it is not clear whether these differences were related to Einstein’s exceptional intellectual abilities.

    The whereabouts of most of Einstein’s brain are unknown, but some samples are still being studied by researchers.

    Einstein Death Cause

    Albert passed away on April 18, 1955, at the age of 76. The cause of his death was internal bleeding caused by the rupture of an aortic aneurysm.

    This is a condition in which the wall of the aorta, the main artery carrying blood from the heart, weakens and bulges, creating a potential risk for rupture as well as internal bleeding.

    He had been in poor health in the years leading up to his death as well, as the rupture of the aneurysm was ultimately what led to his passing.
