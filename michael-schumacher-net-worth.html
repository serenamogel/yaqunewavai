Michael Schumacher: Houses, Cars, Island, and Private Jet

Houses

Michael Schumacher, along with his wife, has purchased numerous real estate across the world.

At the time when the unfortunate accident happened, Michael and his wife Corinna lived in a mansion in Switzerland located on the coast of Lake Geneva.

The $35 million worth mansion has 40 rooms in total. It has more than 22,000 square feet of living space with a 29 car garage, helipad, and a football field.

The mansion features every luxurious amenities imaginable, including a home theater and a bowling alley.

Similarly, the Schumachers also own another villa around the Lake Geneva area.

The property’s been listed in the market but has not found an interested buyer yet. However, Michael has offered the villa for a rent of $26,700.

Moreover, Michael also owns a horse ranch in the United States. The ranch is located in Gordonville, Texas.

The CS Ranch boasts 26 horse stalls and also serves as a venue for international reining competitions.

As one might expect, the area has international standards and measures 40 meters in width and 95 meters in length. Moreover, it has a capacity of 1,400 spectators.

Apart from that, Michael’s spouse purchased a property in Majorca, Spain, for $40 million that spans over 16,000 square feet.

Michael Schumacher: Lifestyle

Michael Schumacher lives an opulent lifestyle along with his wife and children.

He got married in August 1995 to Corinna Betsch. The couple has two children Gina Marie and Mick Schumacher.

Though he is a global superstar, he likes to keep his personal life out of the limelight, far from the paparazzi and media.

Before his unfortunate accident, he enjoyed playing football for his local teal FC Echichens and horse riding.

Michael was announced a driver for the Ferrari Driving Academy in 2019.

Schumacher’s career was cut short after a terrible skiing accident in December 2013, leaving him with a brain injury.

He went out skiing with his son Mick in the French Alps when he fell and hit his head on a rock.

Though he was wearing his helmet, he severely injured his head. Fortunately, he was wearing a helmet, or else he would have died on the spot.

Following the accident, he was put down in a medically induced coma for more than six months.

However, in June 2014, he regained consciousness and was transferred to rehab in Switzerland.

Similarly, he was finally brought home for recovery in September 2014. However, he was paralyzed and in a wheelchair in November 2014. The racer suffered memory loss and was unable to speak.

Michael was transported to Paris to undergo an advanced stem cell treatment.

As of 2021, Michael is in a vegetative state, meaning he’s awake but does not respond.

His heart is beating, he can breathe and can probably sit up, but that’s it.

Michael Schumacher: Career

Michael Schumacher began racing at a very early age and was considered the most successful and greatest Formula One driver of all time.

In 1991, Michael made his Formula One debut at the Belgian Grand Prix.

He joined the Ferrari SPA racing team in 1996. After Michael joined the team, the long struggle of the team Ferrari came to an end as he began winning races practically overnight.

Michael was unstoppable with Ferrari and really hit it off when he won the prestigious Constructors title in 1999.

He was having time of his life with numerous wins and championship titles, and in the late 90s, his career skyrocketed with more successful and lucrative wins.

He became a driver to win more championships than any other driver in the racing history in the 2000s.

Michael won seven world driver’s championships, with the last four in a row beginning from 2000 to 2003.

Similarly, the racer also won the Japanese Grand Prix and Italian Grand Prix.

However, around 2005 his success level dropped, and he announced his retirement in 2006 only to come back with Mercedes GP in 2010.

With Mercedes, he finished third at the European Grand Prix in 2012.

After the terrible skiing accident in 2013, Michael Schumacher has been resting with severe brain damage.
